1. Can't Help Myself

Can't Help Myself was created by Sun Yuan and Pengyu in 2016. According to an article from Guggenheim New York - where the artwork was commissioned from - the idea of using a robotic arm as the main focus allude to the artists' desire replace their 'artistic will' with something mechanically programmed. There is a deeper meaning to the work in regard to surveillance which is further expanded in articles covering this work.

I first heard about Can't Help Myself when it went viral on TikTok a few years ago and then again when it was brought up during the class tutorial. Although I haven't made up my mind on if I 'like' this work or not, one thing that really impressed me is how Sun Yuan and Pengyu are able to depict a non-sentient robot in such a human way that is almost grotesque. I really respect the execution of this work and the emotions that it evoke.

2. Silver Swan

Automatons are machines that attempt to imitate organic beings. They were especially popular during the late 1800s and early 1900s when they were considered to be a novelty for entertainment. Silver Swan is considered to be one of the most well known automatons due to its exceptionally intricate craftsmanship and realism.

There's a certain whimsicality and magicalness to automatons that I'm quite fond of. Eventhough it's technically more mechanical engineering than physical computing as they are powered by clockwork, I think that there's a lot that can be learnt from automatons - particularly in how they present motion and different material textures.

3. Edoardo Landi

Edoardo Landi is an Italian artist specialising in kinetic art. His works often involve the physical manipulation of geometric shapes and forms to perceptually stimulate the viewer.

There are limited footages of his works on the internet which makes my grasp of them feel incomplete. However, I was intrigued by the fact that a lot of his works look like something which could have been coded in p5.js. It made me see the potential of translating generative or code-based art into physical objects.
