1. Password Journals

The computer lock-based password journal was a popular toy marketed to young girls in the early 2000s. It was advertised with ‘high security’ features like voice-activated locks, colour screens, and even built-in cameras. These features gave the diary a sense of high-tech security, framing it as something completely personal and uniquely yours. I was intrigued by the idea of technology being integrated into something intimate, like a diary, and how it transforms a simple object into something that feels magical.

Looking back now, it’s clear how much of this product was just gimmick, but that’s also what makes it so interesting. It speaks to the way tech can be both ephemeral and meaningful, particularly in the context of growing up. There's something poetic about how the look and feel of a certain era’s technology—no matter how limited—can become a marker of understanding the world, or at least a milestone in how we experienced it at the time.

2. Michael Manning

Michael Manning is a mysterious guy that I found in an obscure corner of the internet. There’s very little documentation about him, and as far as I can tell, a single Reddit post compiling links to his work is the only accessible source online.

Despite the mystery, I found his work really interesting. He creates very simple webpages, often built around a single visual effect or transition. While minimal, they’re a great reference for small, modular ideas that could be incorporated into a larger internet art piece which I'm leaning towards doing for my final project.

3. Victor Arce

Victor Arce works within a very specific Y2K aesthetic that blends elements of vaporwave and maximalism. His visuals combine 2D and 3D media to result in complex, multi-layered compositions.

To me, Victor's works has an aesthetic that feels nostalgic yet futuristic, and one that offers a lot of inspiration in terms of both style and technique.
